Women ex-offenders who formally lived in Chicago public housing will get a second chance to get supportive housing under a plan announced this week by the Chicago Housing Authority Board of Commissioners.

At their Aug. 16 public meeting at the Savoy Square Community Center, 4448 S. State St., the CHA Board authorized interim Chief Executive Officer Carlos Ponce to contract with the Viceroy Apartments on the Near West Side to set aside 17 units of subsidized housing for formerly incarcerated women who once lived in public housing.

The Viceroy Apartments is a six-story brick hotel that was constructed in the 1920s and is located at 1519 W. Warren Blvd. Heartland Housing is redeveloping the structure to include historic preservation of the façade and a total rehab of the interior and its 89 studio apartments, according to an Aug. 10 letter from CHA staff to the board that was e-mailed to Residents’ Journal. The letter indicates that 72 units in the building will for homeless women or those at risk of being homeless. The CHA waiting list will be the primary source of referrals for these 72 units. The remaining 17 units will be master-leased to St. Leonard’s Ministries to provide permanent supportive housing to women ex-offenders. The City of Chicago is donating the property, while Low Income Housing Tax Credits, Tax Incremental Financing (TIF) funds and other sources will be used to complete the rehab. Rent will be set at approximately $685 per month.

CHA Chair Explains

CHA Board Chairman James Reynolds explained to Residents’ Journal after the meeting why the board members decided to provide supportive housing to the women ex-offenders: “This is an area of the population that really needs supportive housing. As far as we are concerned, they paid any debt that they owed to society, and we owe them the same support that we give other citizens in this city.”

Reynolds added that CHA is also working on providing supportive housing to male ex-offenders in the future. “I can tell you that we’re going to seek to expand this,” he said.

Praise from an Ex-offenders’ Advocate

Willie J.R. Fleming, a member of the Chicago Anti-Eviction Campaign and a co-founder of the Campaign to Restore National Housing Rights, praised Reynolds and other CHA board members on providing subsidized housing for the women ex-offenders during the board meeting.

The Viceroy Hotel project “is a step in the right direction,” Fleming told RJ. “It’s a small step. But it’s a big step. The Chicago Housing Authority responded in a timely fashion, in a manner that would probably make the President of the United States happy. Knowing that he is going under a lot of conflict right now, and now he’s got an initiative that can sweep across the country.”

Fleming added that he and his group were instrumental in bringing the issue to light in meetings with the Congressional Black Caucus and the Congressional Progressive Caucus as well as federal housing officials: “We made re-entry, ex-offenders’ right to return to public housing, one of key issues. So whenever we were in Washington, D.C., engaging with the secretary of HUD Shaun Donavon, we kept pushing the envelope on ex-offenders having a right to return.”

Fleming said that he and other members of his human rights group were “proud to be in Chicago, because we are one of the first cities to implement a program that will allow formerly incarcerated women back into the public housing structure.”

Fleming added that they also believe that the new initiative is going to stop some of the violence in the city’s low-income communities. “These mothers have a chance to get their children back under the roof with them and raise them in the correct manner,” he said.

Fleming also commended Ald. Walter Burnett (27) for his part in the new housing initiative. “As an ex-felon, he knows about ex-offenders’ rights,” he said.

According to current federal law for nationwide public housing occupancy, ex-felons are not allowed to rent any public housing units. But the CHA is looking for ways to rectify that situation, according to Fleming.

“Now the CHA will also look at a way where they can really get ex-offenders back into public housing,” he said. “Maybe they can create some type of pilot program that allows for probation, things of that nature. But to see that they have an interest in it, and to see that they are committed to work on it, it means a lot to the residents, and the people of this city.”
